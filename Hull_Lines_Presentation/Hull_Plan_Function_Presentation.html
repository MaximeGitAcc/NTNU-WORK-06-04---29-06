I] Offsets Table

The offset table is a table that helps describe the shape of a ship's hull in three dimensions.It's devided in two parts.

Parts of the table of offsets :

Half Breadth :
Half Breadth refer to the horizontal distances between the ship's centerline (longitudinal center) and the exterior surface of the hull at different stations and waterlines. This part of the offset table describes the lateral shape of the hull. These lines are used to draw the half Breadths plan and the body plan.

Heights :
Heights refer to the vertical distances from the base plane of the ship (often the keel) to the outer surface of the hull at various stations and buttock lines. This part of the offset table describes the vertical shape of the hull. These lines are used to draw the sheer plan

Content of the table of offsets :

In Column, Stations:
The columns represent stations, which are vertical cross sections of the hull at different points along the length of the ship.

Waterlines in Lines:
Waterlines represent horizontal levels on the hull, measured at various heights from the keel or bottom of the ship. They are often spaced at regular intervals, such as every foot or meter.

Buttocklines:
Buttock lines are vertical lines parallel to the centerline of the ship, taken at regular intervals on either side of this line. They make it possible to describe the longitudinal shape of the hull in terms of height.

II] Half Breadth Plan

The Half Breadth Plan is an essential representation in marine construction, describing the lateral shape of the hull by half-breadths measured at specific stations and waterlines. It is crucial for the precise design, construction and verification of hull form, ensuring that the vessel has the desired characteristics in terms of performance and stability.


Figure No. 3: Balclutha Half Breadth Plan. [3]

To draw this chart on an html page, I went through a JS script that uses the chart.js library. With this library we can create a modular graph, thanks to different properties. Of course first of all the abscissas and ordinates, but also the legend, the labels of the axes, properties of the plot, display or not the points on the plot, title and the fact that it is responsive or not.

III] Sheer Plan

The sheer plan, also called a profile plan or longitudinal elevation plan, is an essential technical drawing in shipbuilding. It represents the side (profile) view of a ship. This plan shows the outline and main lines of the ship as they appear when viewed from the side, from bow to stern.

IV] Body Plan

The body plan, also called cross-section plan or template plan, is another essential technical drawing in shipbuilding. It represents the cross sections of a ship's hull, viewed from the front (bow) and rear (stern). This plan shows the shape and contours of the hull at different positions along the length of the ship.

Conclusion

The functions presented allow for the calculation of characteristic values of a boat's hull based on data present in a JSON file. The coefficient values could not be verified, and the quality of the results greatly depends on the accuracy of the HalfBreadths table. The more points provided, the more accurate the calculations will be.
However, if the Draft value lies between two waterlines, a new waterline is created by interpolation. This helps to make the values of the submerged volume, Cw, and the coordinates of the Buoyancy point more accurate.
